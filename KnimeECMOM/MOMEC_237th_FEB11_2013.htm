Item No.3 Any other item with the permission of the Chair.

a) IBA Summer School Program – 2013.

3.1 Ms. Maheen Ghauri said that the IBA Summer School Program has been conceived by drawing up the experiences of other Summer Schools at Harvard, Stanford, Cambridge, UCLA, BU, LSE, Indian Bangalore Summer Programs, King’s College, Kellogg, and Monash. The concept is to develop and sustain a wide-range of beneficial and innovative academic and recreational courses and programs for a diverse population of students and adults alike that would benefit them in their professional and personal development.

3.2 Ms. Maheen Ghauri said that most summer schools offer a wide variety of summer courses that can range from 1 week (short term) courses to 6 weeks (long term) courses. Summer schools offer more than just academic courses. For instance, self-development and recreational courses such as reading, creative writing, photography, sketching, music, dancing, women entrepreneurship, SAT & GRE preparation may be offered. She said that in Pakistan, the Summer School concept is still novel in the Higher Education segment. LUMS has launched summer camps, but there are still no summer schools developed or launched based on the pattern of the above-mentioned universities.

3.3 She said that currently, IBA has been offering business courses every summer to individuals including current students, undergraduates and graduates from other universities, and adults. By offering academic as well as self-development and recreational courses in the IBA Summer School Program, the students would learn from eminent faculty and instructors who are specialists in their field, through a stimulating experience that would augment their academic, professional, and personal lives.

3.4 The IBA Summer School’s primary emphasis is to enable individuals to develop and enrich their academic, professional, and personal lives. Thus, the Summer School will design, develop, and offer courses that benefit a diverse audience within and outside Pakistan. The key objectives of the IBA Summer School Program include:

• To introduce, develop, and launch the IBA Summer School Program by March 2013
• To develop 1 week (short term) courses to 6 weeks (long term) courses that can be offered to a diverse group of candidates
• To provide IBA faculty with teaching opportunities that allow them to apply innovative teaching techniques and pedagogy within the classroom
• To invite well-known and experienced visiting faculty from diverse fields to conduct short courses at IBA
• To collaborate with the CEE and CED to design short courses that can be taught at the summer school.
• To provide a stimulating learning environment through a range of innovative academic, self-development, and recreational courses taught by prominent faculty from and outside IBA.
• To promote IBA as one of the top schools in Pakistan, so as to attract people from other cities and countries to join our diverse degree programs
• To increase the enrolment in the IBA Summer School to at least 1,500 students in Year 1.

ELIGIBILTY CRITERIA

3.5 Ms. Maheen Ghauri said that the following is the proposed eligibility criteria:

• Students currently enrolled in A-levels (high school) or Intermediate (college) in any discipline
• Students enrolled in an institute of higher education
• Adults with a minimum qualification of Intermediate of A-levels
• Current IBA undergraduate, graduate, or PhD students

3.6 The Dean & Director said that he and the Board are both concerned that we must have a financial sustainability model because so many buildings will be ready and the recurring expenditures on maintenance would require huge amount, so capacity utilization will have to be maximized. He said that we have endowment fund and from its income we will partially finance but we have to find other sources of income from non-regular programs. CEE, CED, CBER have to provide supplementary revenues to augment our regular programs. He said that many universities have thriving Summer Schools that bring significant revenues. We should start with those courses for which we have the expertise. Highlighting the benefits of this program he said that the existing teachers can enhance their income by teaching in this program; our capacity utilization will increase, and revenue will also rise. He said that we would try to use the existing courses and modify them in order to cater to the demand. He said that we have to make sure that the brand is not diluted in any way and there would be no compromise on the contents and quality. Even in non-academic courses we have to maintain certain quality and we have to build quality assurance mechanism into it. After presenting the motivation and concept of this program, the Dean & Director sought feedback from the EC members.

3.7 To a question, the Dean & Director clarified that the academic courses for our regular students would continue to be offered in the evening as is presently done but the non-academic courses would be offered in the morning.

3.8 Mirza Izhar Husain suggested that the Summer Program courses should be announced much in advance so that students abroad may know well in time. Mr. Zafar Siddiqui suggested that the target market should be high school and ‘O’ level students. The Dean & Director said that we can inform Pakistani schools, Pakistan embassies, and Pakistani associations outside Pakistan.

3.9 Dr. Muhammad Nishat said that some students coming from abroad may need credit of the courses because that credit is transferred. The Dean & Director said that they would see that later when demand for credit is presented to us formally. At this moment it will be a certificate course.

3.10 The Dean & Director suggested to offer this for both ‘O’ level and ‘A’ level students, and keep it more flexible, broad based. He also suggested to see the programs offered by IMS Peshawar, Indus Valley School, PACC, Goethe etc. for guidance.

DECISION

3.11 As some members could not go through the proposal, it was decided that Ms. Maheen Ghouri would present the proposal again incorporating the above suggestions.

c) To consider request from Mr. Asad Salman Malik, a student of BBA-II.

3.15 Mr. Asad Salman Malik, a student of BBA-II in his email stated that he took a course of Foundation of Human Behavior with Dr. Erum Maqbool in the first semester. He stated that he was not shown the papers or given marks of the second hourly till one week after final exam. Due to this he dropped another course (Calculus-I) as he was given its second hourly marks within time and he realized that he was not doing well in that course while thinking that his second hourly marks of Human Behaviour would be satisfactory. But when he was given the second hourly Human Behaviour marks a month later (after the final exam), his marks were not so as he had expected. He could not do anything about dropping the course or improving his grade in the final either as he had already dropped Calculus-I. He stated that when he was given the Human Behaviour final exam result, and due to not being able to improve his grade in the final (as he had not received his second hourly marks while giving his final exam), he failed the course by 3.25 marks. Due to this ‘F’ his first semester GPA is falling below 2.0 resulting in his drop out from IBA right after his first semester. His contention was that if he would have been shown the second hourly marks in time, he would have dropped Human Behaviour instead of Calculus-I and would have passed Calculus-I. He requested to allow him to drop Foundations of Human Behaviour and repeat the course in the summer semester instead of grading him with an ‘F’ and dropping him out of IBA.

3.16 Dr. Muhammad Nishat said that he was told that Dr. Erum Maqbool, who is a visiting faculty, was very busy so she could not submit the result on time. The Dean & Director asked Dr. Nishat to penalize Dr. Erum Maqbool by deducting Rs.5000/- per day for the period she delayed the result.

3.17 Mr. Mobin Khalili said that many students knew their result and he gave them chance to withdraw from the course and many of them withdrew. He said that it is not IBA’s fault it is individual’s fault. He said that withdrawal of one course is allowed and that Mr. Asad Malik has already done. He said that the student has already been dropped.

3.18 The Dean & Director said that the chairpersons and the Associate Deans should ensure that the teacher submit the result on time, failing which penalty must be imposed. We have given ample opportunities to the delinquent teachers but there has been no visible improvement in the posting of exam results. The students suffer due to this casual and lax attitude of a few among us. He said that the Examination Department had informed the students about their result and the students were given the opportunity to withdraw from the course. Mr. Asad Malik had already withdrawn from a course so he could not withdraw from another course.

DECISION

3.19 In view of the facts stated by the Controller of Examinations as stated in 3.16 above, the Committee did not accede to the request of Mr. Asad Salman Malik.

3.20 The Committee decided that a penalty of Rs.5000/- per day should be imposed on faculty member who is delinquent in posting the examination results after the given deadline.

ACTION

3.21 Dr. Muhammad Nishat would convey the decision to Mr. Asad Salman Malik. He would also impose penalty on Dr. Erum Maqbool for late submission of the result.

3.22 The Associate Deans would notify the faculty members about the penalty for late submission of exam results.
